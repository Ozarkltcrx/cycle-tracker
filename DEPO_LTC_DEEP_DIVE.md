# DEPO LTC Deep Dive

_Date: 2026-03-10_

## Purpose
This memo expands the earlier LTC trends note with more operational depth, especially where the trends matter to a Missouri long-term care pharmacy. The goal is not just to describe the market, but to translate industry movement into **where margin, workload, service risk, and account opportunity are likely to shift**.

---

## Executive view
The LTC industry is no longer just dealing with a generic “post-pandemic recovery.” It is moving into a more durable operating model defined by five realities:

1. **Residents are sicker and more medication-intensive than before.**
2. **Facilities are still labor-constrained even when headline staffing has improved.**
3. **Payment remains structurally tight, especially for Medicaid-heavy buildings.**
4. **Regulatory exposure is pushing operators toward more auditable workflows.**
5. **Pharmacies are being evaluated less as dispensers and more as operating partners.**

For a Missouri LTC pharmacy, this means the next stage of competition is likely to be won on:
- admission execution,
- exception management,
- survey/compliance support,
- clinical risk reduction,
- and the ability to reduce nursing friction in a Medicaid-heavy environment.

The broad industry trend is that **every med-related defect now carries more downstream cost** than it used to: more nurse callbacks, more charting burden, more consultant follow-up, more survey exposure, more family dissatisfaction, and more rehospitalization risk.

---

## 1) What has actually changed in LTC operations

### A. Occupancy recovery does not equal operating recovery
Many operators have seen occupancy improve from pandemic troughs, but that does not mean their economics have normalized.

What this means in practice:
- Buildings can be “full enough” to feel busy while still being margin-thin.
- A higher census often brings more admissions, readmissions, and order changes before the building has fully rebuilt its clinical infrastructure.
- Pharmacy demand rises with occupancy, but so does service volatility.

**Operator reality:** a building at better occupancy but with unstable nursing leadership, heavy agency use, or poor admission coordination may actually be harder for pharmacy to serve well than a lower-census but well-run building.

### B. The resident mix is heavier, not just larger
Facilities are taking care of more residents with:
- polypharmacy,
- dementia and behavioral symptoms,
- diabetes complexity,
- anticoagulation risk,
- renal impairment,
- wound/infection issues,
- high-risk pain regimens,
- and frequent post-hospital medication changes.

This is a major pharmacy trend because higher acuity increases:
- new-start volume,
- dose-change volume,
- clarification calls,
- controlled substance handling,
- antibiotic stewardship needs,
- consultant pharmacist intervention workload,
- and risk from delayed first doses.

**Practical implication:** even if Rx count growth looks modest, the labor required per resident is often higher than it was several years ago.

### C. Length of stay compression creates churn
Post-acute and managed care pressures continue to shorten stays for many skilled residents. That means more throughput.

For pharmacy, throughput drives:
- more admissions/discharges per occupied bed,
- more reconciliation activity,
- more partial fills and stop/start waste,
- more urgent delivery moments,
- and more opportunities for order mismatch across hospital, SNF, and prescriber records.

In other words, the business strain is not only "how many beds are served" but **how many medication events each bed generates**.

---

## 2) The labor problem has evolved from shortage to fragility
The industry narrative is often "staffing shortage," but the more operator-relevant description is **staffing fragility**.

A building may technically meet staffing needs on paper and still operate in a brittle way because of:
- call-offs,
- agency dependence,
- inexperienced nurses,
- rotating agency med-pass staff,
- DON/admin turnover,
- and weak follow-through on documentation or QA.

### Why this matters to pharmacy
When nurse time is scarce or inconsistent, every pharmacy issue gets more expensive:
- refill misses become crisis calls,
- unclear orders sit longer,
- prior auth delays become floor-level disruptions,
- med cart stockouts create nurse workarounds,
- and consultant recommendations are less likely to be operationalized.

The pharmacy service model that wins in this environment is not simply “accurate dispensing.” It is **fewer exceptions, faster exception closure, and workflows that survive understaffed buildings**.

### What operators increasingly value from pharmacy under labor pressure
- Fewer inbound nurse phone calls needed to resolve routine issues
- Cleaner cycle-fill performance
- Packaging that supports faster med pass
- Predictable admission turnaround, especially after hours
- Actionable recommendations instead of long consultant note dumps
- Reporting that helps admin/DON teams find the highest-risk issues fast

**Bottom line:** labor scarcity increases the value of pharmacy reliability, but it especially increases the value of pharmacy simplification.

## 3) The reimbursement picture still drives behavior at the building level
### A. Medicaid remains the structural stress point
MACPAC previously estimated that the **median Medicaid base payment rate was 86% of reported facility costs** nationally, with large variation across facilities and states. That is directionally important even when local supplemental payments complicate the exact picture.

The operating takeaway is simple: many buildings still behave like organizations that cannot afford much friction.

That affects pharmacy because facilities become more sensitive to:
- total med spend,
- non-covered/exception handling,
- waste from short stays and therapy changes,
- inventory tied up on shelves,
- and service failures that consume nurse/admin labor.

### B. Medicare and managed care do not fully offset the problem
Even when post-acute mix improves, operators are still dealing with:
- payer authorization burden,
- shorter covered stays,
- more utilization scrutiny,
- readmission sensitivity,
- and clinical expectations that resemble acute care discharge management.

So while Medicare/MA residents can be financially important, they also raise the bar on pharmacy responsiveness.

### C. Missouri-specific reimbursement signal worth noting
The Missouri MO HealthNet nursing facility rate list for SFY 2026 provides a few useful signals:
- Missouri publicly posts a **Nursing Facility Rate List** for participating facilities.
- The report date on the current SFY 2026 workbook is **8/1/2025**.
- It states the **7/1/2025 rates were not final** as of that report date.
- It also notes the published 7/1/2025 rates **did not yet include a $0.38 increase in the VBP incentive**, pending CMS approval of a State Plan Amendment.

Why that matters:
- It shows that even small per-diem adjustments are operationally important enough to be called out.
- It reinforces that Missouri operators are working inside a payment environment where **small reimbursement timing changes matter**, especially in Medicaid-heavy facilities.
- A pharmacy cannot assume customers have much room to absorb avoidable med-related cost, waste, or labor burden.

## 4) Missouri operating lens: what the public rate file suggests
I reviewed Missouri’s public MO HealthNet nursing facility rate workbook (SFY 2026) and extracted directional observations.

### Directional takeaways from the file
- The workbook listed **530 participating nursing facilities**.
- Using the file’s location labels, about **277 were marked urban** and about **200 rural**; the balance appeared unlabeled or otherwise not cleanly categorized in the simple extraction.
- The published **7/1/2025 per-diem rates showed wide spread**, with extracted values ranging roughly from **$162/day to $469/day**, and a median around **$244/day**.
- In the extraction, **urban facilities showed higher average published rates and higher average case-mix indices than rural facilities**.
- Facilities with very high Medicaid bed concentration were common, reinforcing how Medicaid-heavy much of the state’s nursing facility base is.

### Operator implications for Missouri LTC pharmacy
#### Rural Missouri
Typical concerns are likely to include:
- thinner staffing pipelines,
- more delivery/logistics sensitivity,
- fewer local clinical backup resources,
- slower response loops for clarifications,
- and greater vulnerability when one nurse leader or one strong med-tech leaves.

For pharmacy, rural account strategy should emphasize:
- delivery reliability and contingency planning,
- emergency box / first-dose readiness,
- standardization over customization,
- proactive refill governance,
- and simple reporting that a stretched admin can actually use.

#### Urban Missouri / major metro accounts
Likely patterns include:
- higher acuity,
- more post-acute churn,
- more hospital discharge volume,
- more payer complexity,
- and more competitive pressure around service level and responsiveness.

For pharmacy, urban strategy should emphasize:
- admission speed,
- reconciliation depth,
- faster order clarification,
- higher-touch clinical interventions,
- and account-level service analytics.

#### Across Missouri overall
The state appears to present a classic split market:
- **rural/logistics-intensive operations**, and
- **urban/churn-and-acuity-intensive operations**.

That argues against a one-size-fits-all service model.

## 5) Regulation and survey pressure: where medication operations stay exposed
### A. Medication management remains survey-sensitive
Regardless of any specific enforcement cycle, medication-related risk remains concentrated around a familiar set of topics:
- psychotropics and unnecessary medications,
- gradual dose reduction and supporting documentation,
- antibiotic stewardship,
- controlled substance accountability,
- pain management documentation,
- timeliness/accuracy of med pass,
- med error follow-up,
- and resident rights/informed consent issues.

### B. Why survey pressure matters more now than it used to
Survey problems are more expensive in a fragile operating environment because they can trigger:
- corrective action work,
- leadership distraction,
- referral damage,
- payer confidence issues,
- more consultant time,
- and staff morale problems.

Pharmacy can add real value if it helps buildings move from reactive cleanup to routine surveillance.

### C. CMS minimum staffing rule remains strategically important
The exact legal and implementation path may continue to shift, but the big-picture implication remains: operators expect staffing expectations and public scrutiny to tighten, not loosen.

That matters to pharmacy because facilities will increasingly prefer vendors that help them:
- save nurse minutes,
- reduce med-pass disruption,
- produce cleaner documentation,
- and operate more predictably with fewer workarounds.

**Important point:** even if minimum staffing requirements are delayed, challenged, or modified, operator behavior has already changed. Buildings are planning around a world of higher staffing visibility.

## 6) Clinical trend lines that matter most to LTC pharmacy economics
### A. Psychotropic stewardship is still high stakes
This area stays important because it sits at the intersection of:
- survey risk,
- family concern,
- prescriber oversight,
- dementia-care complexity,
- and nursing burden.

Pharmacy opportunity:
- flagged psychotropic dashboards,
- documentation support for indication/monitoring/GDR review,
- behavior-med review workflows tied to monthly QA,
- and targeted consultant follow-up on outlier buildings.

### B. Antibiotic stewardship has become an operations problem, not just a clinical one
In LTC, antibiotic management is tied to infection control, culture follow-up, renal dosing, stop dates, and hospital transfer avoidance.

Pharmacy value is highest when it helps buildings avoid:
- open-ended antibiotic courses,
- inappropriate duplicate coverage,
- missed renal adjustments,
- and treatment plans that create confusion across shifts.

### C. Diabetes and anticoagulation remain reliability tests
These are not new categories, but they remain potent because errors or delays have immediate clinical consequences.

Common pain points:
- sliding-scale or insulin-change communication gaps,
- discharge med mismatch,
- held orders without clear restart logic,
- lab/result follow-up gaps,
- and inconsistent monitoring documentation.

Buildings notice quickly which pharmacy helps make these categories safer versus which one creates more follow-up work.

### D. Controlled substances remain both a compliance and labor issue
The operational burden includes:
- receipt/signature handling,
- count discrepancies,
- emergency supply expectations,
- after-hours prescribing and delivery coordination,
- and diversion concern.

A pharmacy that reduces controlled-sub workflow pain becomes sticky, especially in understaffed buildings.

## 7) Technology trend: the best tools are the ones that remove rework
LTC operators are increasingly skeptical of broad technology promises and more interested in narrow, provable ROI.

### Tools/workflows most likely to matter
- refill automation that actually reduces exceptions,
- order-entry interfaces that reduce duplicate work,
- eMAR alignment and status visibility,
- admission packet/order intake standardization,
- prior-authorization tracking,
- consultant worklists that prioritize highest-value reviews,
- exception dashboards for late fills / missing signatures / psychotropics / antibiotics / controls.

### What this means for pharmacy strategy
The most defendable tech investments are not the flashiest. They are the ones that:
- lower nurse interruption volume,
- reduce clarification loops,
- cut missed-dose events,
- speed admissions,
- and generate auditable evidence of service value.

A practical way to think about pharmacy tech in LTC:
- **Tier 1:** prevent workflow failures
- **Tier 2:** shorten the time to close failures that do happen
- **Tier 3:** create reporting that proves the pharmacy is helping operations

If a tool does not move one of those three, it may not matter enough.

## 8) Account segmentation: not all facilities should receive the same pharmacy model
A major theme across LTC is that customer needs diverge more than they used to.

### Segment 1: Stable long-stay / lower-churn buildings
Characteristics:
- lower admission velocity,
- more routine cycle-fill needs,
- fewer rapid therapy changes,
- often highly Medicaid-exposed.

Best pharmacy offer:
- low-friction fulfillment,
- packaging efficiency,
- med-cart optimization,
- refill accuracy,
- survey/compliance support.

### Segment 2: High-churn skilled/post-acute buildings
Characteristics:
- frequent admissions and readmissions,
- therapy turnover,
- more hospital-origin medication complexity,
- stronger need for after-hours readiness.

Best pharmacy offer:
- aggressive admission SLAs,
- reconciliation support,
- rapid first-dose capability,
- clinical escalation support,
- analytics around turnaround time and defects.

### Segment 3: Behavioral / memory-care-heavy buildings
Characteristics:
- psychotropic scrutiny,
- behavior documentation issues,
- family sensitivity,
- need for stable med administration workflows.

Best pharmacy offer:
- psychotropic stewardship package,
- consultant pharmacist targeting,
- GDR and indication review support,
- monthly outlier reporting.

### Segment 4: Operationally weak buildings
Characteristics:
- frequent leadership turnover,
- high nurse callback volume,
- persistent documentation issues,
- repeated refill/admission friction.

Best pharmacy offer:
- more structured onboarding,
- tighter refill governance,
- named escalation paths,
- templated reports and action plans,
- clear account service boundaries so weak operations do not consume unlimited pharmacy labor.

**Key point:** some buildings need partnership; some need guardrails.

## 9) Where LTC pharmacy margin is most likely to erode next
If current trends continue, the biggest hidden margin leaks are likely to be:

1. **Admission chaos**
   - unstructured intake,
   - unclear hospital discharge orders,
   - repeated clarification calls,
   - rush delivery and repack labor.

2. **Short-stay waste**
   - therapy changes,
   - discontinued meds,
   - returned or unusable product,
   - packaging not matched to LOS reality.

3. **Nurse-driven exception volume**
   - each missing refill or unclear order becomes expensive when it interrupts limited floor staff.

4. **Clinical complexity without targeted triage**
   - not every high-risk resident needs the same level of pharmacy attention, but some absolutely do.

5. **Over-servicing weak accounts without operational discipline**
   - pharmacy teams can unintentionally subsidize disorganized buildings through repeated handholding that is never systematized.

6. **Consulting effort that is not tied to action**
   - long recommendations that do not change facility behavior create work without account defense.

## 10) What smart operators will increasingly ask of pharmacy partners
Expect better operators to increasingly evaluate pharmacies on questions like:
- How fast do you handle admissions, really?
- What percentage of our nurse callbacks are preventable?
- Can you show me where our med-related survey risk is rising?
- Can you help my DON see psychotropic, antibiotic, and controlled-med outliers in one place?
- What are you doing to reduce missed-dose or late-fill incidents?
- How do you support a building on weekends, nights, and readmits?
- What are you doing to lower med-pass burden for nurses?
- Can you segment service by my building type instead of giving every site the same package?

That is a more sophisticated buying conversation than “Who can fill scripts?”

## 11) Recommended priorities for a Missouri LTC pharmacy operator
### Immediate priorities
1. **Build a Missouri-specific account segmentation model**
   - separate rural/logistics-intensive sites from urban/high-churn sites;
   - tag buildings by Medicaid exposure, acuity, and leadership stability.

2. **Create an admissions command layer**
   - defined intake checklist,
   - order clarification workflow,
   - first-dose service expectations,
   - and after-hours escalation rules.

3. **Launch a survey-risk dashboard**
   - psychotropics,
   - antibiotic duration/outliers,
   - controlled-sub discrepancies,
   - refill misses/late fills,
   - and selected consultant follow-up items.

4. **Reduce nurse interruption volume**
   - measure top callback reasons,
   - remove the most common recurring defects,
   - standardize packaging and refill timing where possible.

5. **Put guardrails around high-friction accounts**
   - define service boundaries,
   - document repeated root causes,
   - and convert heroics into process.

### Medium-term priorities
1. **Formalize hospital-avoidance clinical programs**
   - anticoag review,
   - renal dosing surveillance,
   - antibiotic stewardship,
   - diabetes high-risk resident monitoring,
   - post-discharge med reconciliation.

2. **Turn reporting into account defense**
   - quarterly business reviews should show not just fills, but avoided friction and clinical/compliance support.

3. **Develop different service playbooks for rural vs metro Missouri**
   - rural: reliability, emergency readiness, refill discipline;
   - metro: speed, throughput, reconciliation, analytics.

4. **Tie consultant pharmacist work to operator pain points**
   - survey readiness,
   - behavior-med review,
   - antibiotic clean-up,
   - and action tracking.

### Strategic priorities
1. **Compete on being easier to operate with**
2. **Make data usable for admins and DONs, not just pharmacists**
3. **Use AI/automation narrowly on exception triage and documentation support**
4. **Pursue deeper partnerships with stronger regional operators**
5. **Avoid becoming a generic high-touch vendor with no discipline around cost-to-serve**

---

## 12) Final take
The LTC market is getting more operationally unforgiving.

The core trend is not just higher demand for senior care. It is that nursing facilities are trying to deliver more complex care with less slack, under more scrutiny, in a reimbursement environment that still leaves little room for inefficiency.

For pharmacy, that means the real battleground is no longer dispensing alone. It is the broader med-use system:
- how smoothly residents are admitted,
- how reliably orders are converted into timely therapy,
- how much nurse time is protected,
- how clearly risk is surfaced,
- and how well the building stays out of trouble.

In Missouri specifically, the public MO HealthNet rate structure reinforces a familiar truth: many facilities are highly Medicaid-exposed and sensitive to even small reimbursement changes. That makes pharmacy value more practical and less theoretical. The strongest positioning is likely to come from being the partner that helps facilities **run cleaner, safer, and with fewer interruptions**.
